# Fact Check Report: Claims in "Solving The Money Problem" Transcripts

This document fact-checks several recurring and major claims made by the creator of the "Solving The Money Problem" YouTube channel across multiple videos from February-March 2026.

## 1. BYD's Profitability

**The Claim:** The creator repeatedly claims that BYD is losing money on its vehicle manufacturing business and that its entire profitability is carried solely by its battery cell supply business.
* *Quote:* "To which I would respond, 'Not on their vehicle business, bro. If it weren't for BYD's extremely profitable battery cell supply business, the company would be bleeding billions of dollars per year.'" (2026-02-28)
* *Quote:* "The complete list of companies today selling electric vehicles in high volumes globally and making money doing so... includes just a single name, Tesla." (2026-03-03)

**The Reality:** **FALSE**.
While BYD's profit margins per vehicle have faced downward pressure due to intense domestic price wars in China, BYD remains profitable on its automotive segment. Financial reports consistently show positive gross margins for their vehicle sales. The claim that they are "bleeding billions" on cars and only surviving because of batteries is a common myth circulated by Tesla loyalists to downplay BYD's massive global sales volume (which surpassed Tesla in total units). While battery sales contribute significantly to BYD's overall corporate health, their auto manufacturing is not a loss leader.

## 2. The Tensor Auto "RoboCar"

**The Claim:** The creator dedicates a 20-minute video to hysterically laughing at a presentation by a company called Tensor Auto for a $200,000 personal autonomous vehicle. He suggests the product might be a practical joke or an elaborate fake due to its heavy reliance on buzzwords ("AGI," "Liquid metal skin") and its use of over 100 sensors.
* *Quote:* "I'm not 100% sure if this is legitimate anymore or the world's best ever practical joke..." (2026-03-19)

**The Reality:** **TRUE (The company and pitch exist).**
Tensor Auto is a real startup that unveiled a luxury Level 4 autonomous "RoboCar" intended for private ownership (rather than fleet/robo-taxi use). As the creator noted, it is priced around $200,000 and heavily relies on a massive sensor suite (lidars, radars, cameras). While the creator's mockery of the company's pretentious marketing copy ("obsidian black functional fasia cloaked beneath a liquid metal skin") is arguably justified, the company itself and the presentation he is reacting to are genuine.

## 3. The CarWow Controversy

**The Claim:** The creator alleges that the UK-based automotive YouTube channel CarWow intentionally deceives its audience to make Tesla look bad because Tesla does not pay for advertising or use dealerships (which provide CarWow's revenue). He cites two specific incidents: Matt Watson pretending not to know how to use a Tesla door handle, and CarWow editing a video verdict to remove BYD winning over the Model Y.
* *Quote:* "They literally edited an uploaded video... Our video editor made a mistake with this one and the original verdict didn't reflect Matt's actual thoughts... which was demonstrably a total lie!" (2026-03-20)

**The Reality:** **TRUE (The incidents occurred).**
The incidents the creator references did happen. CarWow faced significant community backlash over a comparison video between the Tesla Model Y and the BYD Sealion. The original video awarded the win to BYD, but after intense negative feedback from viewers (who pointed out the Model Y won most individual categories in the video), CarWow edited the video post-upload to change the winner to Tesla, blaming an "editor's mistake." The creator's assertion that this proves a massive anti-Tesla conspiracy driven by advertising dollars is subjective, but the core events he describes are accurate.

## 4. "Terafab" and "Macrohard" Projects

**The Claim:** Elon Musk announced two massive new initiatives: "Macrohard" (using idle customer Teslas to run AI inference tasks for profit) and "Terafab" (a 100-million-square-foot semiconductor factory built by Tesla/SpaceX/xAI to produce AI chips for deployment in space).
* *Quote (Macrohard):* "Grock is the master conductor... to direct digital optimist which is processing and actioning the past 5 seconds of realtime computer screen video and keyboard and mouse actions... it works in all AI4 equipped cars, so your car can do office work for you when not driving." (2026-03-13)
* *Quote (Terafab):* "Musk chiming in saying that 100 million square feet is the right order of magnitude. Putting that in perspective... three times the size of New York's Central Park." (2026-03-23)

**The Reality:** **TRUE (The announcements occurred, but the viability is highly questionable).**
Elon Musk did announce these projects in March 2026. The "Terafab" project was indeed pitched as a 100-million-square-foot semiconductor fabrication plant aimed at producing chips for orbital data centers. "Macrohard" is also a real proposed initiative to use the compute power of idle Tesla vehicles.
However, while the *announcements* are factually accurate, the creator presents the success of these projects as an absolute, inevitable certainty ("Game [__] over"). The economic and physical realities of building a 100-million-square-foot leading-edge chip fab (which would be the largest building on earth by a massive margin) or running enterprise-grade AI inference reliably on consumer cars parked in residential garages are met with extreme skepticism by industry experts, which the creator dismisses entirely.

## 5. EU Commission Fine on X (Twitter)

**The Claim:** The EU fined X (Twitter) €120 million because they "hate free speech" and want to stop people from verifying their accounts with the blue checkmark.
* *Quote:* "claiming deceptive design of the blue check mark. Yes, that's correct... for what? Allowing people to verify their account." (2026-03-02)

**The Reality:** **FALSE / MISLEADING.**
The creator drastically misrepresents the EU Commission's regulatory actions under the Digital Services Act (DSA). The issue with the blue checkmark was not about "allowing people to verify their account." It was the exact opposite: X changed the blue checkmark from a symbol of verified identity to a symbol that merely indicated a user had paid for a premium subscription. The EU argued this was a "deceptive design" because it confused users who still associated the checkmark with authenticity, making it easier for malicious actors to impersonate public figures and spread disinformation.
